Research Projects

I don't see myself as doing bench-style research, as I prefer regular, and diverse, human contact in a clinical setting. However, when I see a knowledge gap during my online explorations of open-source journal articles, I experience some strong drive to fulfill my curiousity. One such subject is Online Extremism



Online Extremism

Background: One constantly growing area of worry remains among all levels of society: public, private, academic, and government. With the ever-changing digital landscape allowing people to stay hidden (cryptography), remain online (federation), and resistant to take-downs, there is cause for concern among online extremism. Frankly, there are not enough resources to appropriately address the looming, disaffected, crowd of deep, and/or dark-web users.
Aim: However, one approach to this problem would be using data-science to determine fluctuations of high-risk actors, while crossing their online text, with real-world events. This has a few implications. First, by studying the psychology of online extremism, clinicians can develop models of de-escalation for these small populations. Secondly, clinicians will have new tools available for use in identifying individuals who struggle to enact healthy, pro-social, and non-violent means of frustrated expression.
Goals: My project, written in R, uses over 1 billion rows of data, each containing large numbers of words, so that profiles of semantics, n-gram context, and relevant topics (by year(s)) can be used for meeting the aims of the above paragraph.
